Release Before Complete

What would 70 percent of completion look like? Can I ship that?

Why This Matters

The INTP 5w4 mind treats "complete" as a distant, shimmering ideal. The Ti function sees every edge case, every possible improvement, every way the work could be more elegant, more thorough, more perfect. The 4 wing wants the work to be a unique, authentic expression that cannot be mistaken for anyone else's. Together, they form a powerful engine for quality—and a perfect trap for completion. The project that is 90 percent done may never be released, because the remaining 10 percent is infinite. Each improvement reveals two more that could be made. The cathedral is never finished because it is never good enough.

Release Before Complete is the deliberate act of drawing a line. It says: this work is not perfect, and it will never be perfect, and that is acceptable. The standard is not perfection. The standard is usefulness. Is the work legible to my future self? Does it solve the problem it was meant to solve? Can someone else understand it and use it? If the answer is yes, the work is done. The remaining improvements are captured and deferred. The work is released. The cathedral gains a stone. The cycle resets for the next stone.

The Principles

Define "Good Enough" Before Starting

The moment of completion is the worst time to decide what completion means. The Ti function, still holding the model, will argue for more. The Ne will suggest a different approach. The 4 wing will feel that the work lacks the final touch of authenticity. These impulses must be countered by a standard defined in advance, when the mind is calm and the project is still forming. Before I begin the work, I write down: "This is complete when [specific, verifiable condition]." The condition must be binary. It must be visible from the outside. "The manual chapter is complete when all six practices have been written and the navigation links are correct." Not "when it feels right." Not "when it is good enough." When the condition is met, the work is done.

The 70 Percent Standard

Seventy percent is a heuristic, not a precise measurement. It means: what is the smallest version of this work that still fulfills its purpose? The website that works without the final visual polish. The code that compiles and passes tests without the optimized architecture. The document that communicates the core ideas without the final editing pass. The 70 percent version is not sloppy. It is functional. It does the job. The remaining 30 percent—the refinements, the optimizations, the aesthetic flourishes—can be added later, or never, and the work will still have value. The 70 percent version shipped today is worth infinitely more than the 100 percent version that ships never.

Iteration Over Perfection

Releasing before complete is not an act of lowering standards. It is an act of moving the standard from "perfect in isolation" to "improved through engagement." When I release the 70 percent version, I gain something that the 100 percent version could never provide: feedback. The code runs in the real world and reveals bugs I did not anticipate. The writing is read by others and reveals ambiguities I did not see. The system is used and reveals friction points I could not simulate. This feedback is the raw material for the next iteration. The cycle of release, feedback, and improvement produces better work over time than the single, perfect, unreleased artifact ever could.

The Protocol

  1. Before beginning a significant piece of work, write the completion condition. One sentence, binary, verifiable. "This practice file is complete when it includes Why This Matters, The Principles, The Protocol, The Deeper Layer, and Reflection Questions, and the navigation links are functional."
  2. When the condition is met, stop. Do not add one more section. Do not refine one more sentence. The condition is the contract. Honor it.
  3. Capture the deferred improvements. The improvements I notice at the moment of release are not lost. They are captured in the Idea Capture System or a dedicated "future improvements" section of the project. They are promises to the future self, not obligations of the present self.
  4. Release the work. Make it visible. Push the commit. Publish the page. Send the document. The act of releasing is the close of the cycle. It is a deliberate event, not an accidental ending.
  5. If feedback arrives, schedule the next iteration. The feedback is data. Use it to decide whether the deferred improvements are worth pursuing, or whether the next stone of the cathedral takes priority. Not every 70 percent release needs to become a 100 percent release. Some are good enough forever.

The Deeper Layer

Release Before Complete confronts the 5w4's deepest fear about creative work: the fear of being judged as inadequate. The 5 hoards competence and fears exposure. The 4 wing craves recognition but dreads the verdict of "not special enough." Releasing imperfect work feels like inviting the very judgment I have spent a lifetime trying to avoid. But the judgment I fear is almost always harsher than the judgment I receive. The 70 percent version, released into the world, is judged on its actual merits, not on the gap between it and the Platonic ideal in my mind. Most people will not notice the missing 30 percent. They will notice that something exists where before there was nothing.

There is also a Nietzschean dimension. The will to stupidity, applied to completion, means closing my ears to the internal critic that insists the work is not ready. The critic is not wrong. The work is not perfect. The critic is simply not in charge of the release decision. The decision was made when the completion condition was written. The release is the honoring of that prior commitment. The will to stupidity is the strength to ship.

Reflection Questions

  • What is one project I have been holding onto, waiting for it to be "ready"? What would the 70 percent version look like?
  • What is the fear that prevents me from releasing imperfect work? Is it fear of judgment, fear of being ordinary, or something else?
  • When have I released something imperfect and been surprised by the response?
  • What completion condition could I write for my current project that would make release automatic when the condition is met?
