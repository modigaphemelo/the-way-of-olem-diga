The Mask Inventory

How many versions of myself do I present?

Why This Matters

I am not a single, unified self presented consistently to all people. I am a collection of masks—adapted versions of myself, tuned to different audiences and contexts. This is not inherently pathological. All humans adjust their presentation. But for the ASD-1 / INTP configuration, masking is not a minor social lubricant. It is a high-cost, cognitively expensive performance that can consume the majority of my daily energy. And because it has been practiced for so long, often since childhood, I may have lost track of which mask is which, and which face—if any—is the one beneath them all.

The mask inventory is not about eliminating masking. Complete authenticity in all contexts is neither possible nor desirable for this neurotype. The goal is consciousness. I need to know which masks I wear, what they cost me, and which ones serve my sovereignty versus which ones merely placate my fears. I need to be able to choose which mask to wear, rather than having the mask choose itself automatically. And I need to know, with clarity, what lies beneath them all—the core self that is not a performance.

The Common Masks (For This Configuration)

The Competent Analyst

This is the Ti-Ne mask. I present as calm, rational, and deeply knowledgeable. I speak in measured tones, cite evidence, and avoid emotional displays. This mask is deployed in professional settings, intellectual discussions, and anywhere I want to be taken seriously. It is close to my natural cognitive state, but it is still a mask. It suppresses the Fe inferior's need for connection and the 4 wing's emotional intensity. The cost is a sense of isolation and a hunger for genuine recognition that the mask itself prevents.

The Agreeable Companion

This is the Fe inferior mask. I present as warm, accommodating, and easygoing. I nod, I smile, I ask reciprocal questions. I suppress my actual opinions, my need for depth, and my sensory discomfort. This mask is deployed in social situations where I want to avoid conflict, fit in, or simply survive the interaction with minimal friction. The cost is extremely high. Two hours of this mask can require a full day of recovery. Overuse leads to burnout, resentment, and a loss of sense of self.

The Withdrawn Observer

This is the 5's defensive mask. I present as aloof, disinterested, or simply absent. I am physically present but psychologically elsewhere. This mask is deployed when I am depleted, overwhelmed, or protecting myself from demands I cannot meet. It is less a performance and more a retreat. The cost is relational. Others experience this mask as rejection, coldness, or arrogance. Relationships atrophy when this mask is worn too often, even if the intent is self-preservation rather than harm.

The Intense Creator

This is the 4 wing mask. I present as passionate, unique, and deeply engaged with my work or ideas. I speak with intensity about what matters to me. This mask is deployed when I feel safe enough to share my authentic interests, or when I am trying to impress someone with my depth. It is closer to the core self than the Agreeable Companion, but it can still be a performance—an exaggeration of intensity designed to secure the recognition the 4 wing craves. The cost is vulnerability. When this mask is rejected, the wound is deep.

The Minimal Self

This is not a mask but the absence of one. It is what remains when I am alone, depleted, and unable to perform. It is the self that lies in a dark room, staring at the ceiling, with no energy to be anything for anyone. This state is not the "true self" in any aspirational sense. It is the baseline, the depleted core. Knowing this state is important because it reveals what is left when all performance ceases. From this knowledge, I can build a more intentional, less costly set of masks that protect rather than drain this core.

The Protocol

  1. Name my masks. Using the list above as a starting point, write down the masks I recognize in myself. Give each a name that feels accurate. Add any masks not listed. Be honest. This is a private document.
  2. Map the contexts. For each mask, note: When do I wear it? With whom? In what situations? What triggers the switch from one mask to another?
  3. Estimate the cost. For each mask, estimate the energy cost on a scale of 1 to 10. The Agreeable Companion is often an 8 or 9. The Withdrawn Observer may be a 2 in the moment but a 7 in long-term relational damage. The Competent Analyst may be a 4. Be honest about the total cost, not just the immediate effort.
  4. Assess alignment. For each mask, ask: "Does this mask serve my sovereignty, or does it merely placate my fears?" The Competent Analyst may serve sovereignty by allowing me to function in professional settings. The Agreeable Companion, overused, placates the fear of abandonment while draining the resources I need to build.
  5. Identify the core. Beneath all the masks, what remains consistent? What values, hungers, and capacities persist regardless of presentation? This is not a mask to be worn. It is the foundation upon which all masks rest. Write a brief description of this core self. It may be simpler than expected. That is fine.
  6. Choose one mask to adjust. Based on the cost and alignment assessment, identify one mask that is either too costly or misaligned. What is one small adjustment I can make? Example: "I will reduce the duration of the Agreeable Companion mask at social events by leaving after 90 minutes instead of staying until I crash." The goal is not to eliminate the mask. It is to bring it under conscious control.

The Deeper Layer

The mask inventory can trigger a 4 wing crisis. The 4 craves authenticity and despises the idea of being "fake." Discovering that I wear multiple masks, and that some of them are performances designed to please others, can feel like a betrayal of the core identity. "I am not who I thought I was. I am a collection of performances. There is nothing real underneath." This is a misinterpretation. The masks are real. They are strategies I developed to survive and function. The fact that they are performances does not mean the self beneath them is empty. It means the self is adaptable, and it has adapted in ways that served past needs but may not serve present sovereignty.

The ASD dimension adds another layer. Masking is often framed as a burden, a necessary evil imposed by a neurotypical world. This is partly true. But some masking is inevitable in any social existence. The goal is not to eliminate masking. It is to make it conscious, intentional, and less costly. I can choose which masks to wear, for how long, and with what recovery budgeted afterward. This is sovereignty over the social self.

The 5 dimension resists this entire inquiry. The 5 wants to believe it is a unified, rational self, unaffected by social context. The mask inventory reveals this belief as a comforting fiction. Accepting the multiplicity of the self is uncomfortable but liberating. I am not a single, fixed identity. I am a system with multiple modes. Some modes are more aligned with my values than others. Some are more costly than others. I can manage the system consciously, or I can be managed by it unconsciously. The inventory is the first step toward conscious management.

Reflection Questions

  • Which mask do I wear most often? Is it aligned with my sovereignty, or is it primarily fear-driven?
  • Which mask costs me the most energy? What would change if I wore it less frequently or for shorter durations?
  • When I am completely alone and depleted, what remains? What is the core self beneath all performance?
  • What is one small adjustment I can make to one mask this week to reduce its cost or increase its alignment?
