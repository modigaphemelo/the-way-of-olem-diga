Practice 5 of 6

Have My Support Ready

Who is my lawyer, my therapist, my emergency contact?

Why This Matters

Under extreme pressure, the self is not enough. The cognitive resources required to navigate a legal threat, a psychological crisis, or a relational collapse are precisely the resources that are offline during that crisis. The 5's analytical capacity is degraded. The Fe inferior is overwhelmed. The executive function required to research options, make calls, and evaluate advice is depleted. At the moment I most need support, I am least capable of finding it.

Having support ready means establishing the relationships and knowing the procedures before the crisis. It means having a lawyer on retainer or at least identified. It means having a therapist or coach who knows my configuration and can see me quickly. It means having an emergency contact who is not emotionally entangled in my situation and can act as a clear-headed proxy. This is not paranoia. It is the same logic as a fire extinguisher or a spare tire. You do not wait until the fire to buy the extinguisher.

The Support Categories

Legal Support

A lawyer is not just for criminal defense. A lawyer can review contracts before I sign them, advise on tenant or employment disputes, and provide a shield in any interaction where my rights may be at stake. For the ASD/INTP mind, having a lawyer means having someone who speaks the language of systems and procedures, who can translate my concerns into actionable legal positions, and who can be the point of contact when I am too overwhelmed to communicate effectively. I need to identify a lawyer before I need one. This means researching local attorneys, understanding their fee structures, and ideally having an initial consultation so they know who I am. The retainer or consultation fee is insurance, not an expense.

Psychological Support

A therapist, coach, or trusted mentor who understands the INTP 5w4 ASD-1 configuration is essential. This person is not a friend. They are a paid professional whose role is to help me navigate my own mind under pressure. Friends have their own needs, biases, and emotional entanglements. A therapist is a neutral container. They can help me distinguish between a genuine crisis and an Fe inferior overreaction. They can provide the external processing that my Ti-Ne loop cannot achieve alone. I need to establish this relationship before a crisis, because finding a therapist while in crisis is nearly impossible—waiting lists are long, and my executive function is offline.

Emergency Contact (Proxy)

This is the person who can make decisions or communicate on my behalf if I am incapacitated—physically or psychologically. This is not necessarily the person I am closest to emotionally. It is the person who is calm, practical, and understands my wishes. They should have access to essential information: my legal contacts, my medical preferences, my financial basics. This is a formal or informal designation. The key is that they know they have this role and have agreed to it. In a shutdown or overwhelm state, having one person who can say "Olem is not available right now, I'll have him contact you when he's able" is a profound relief.

Financial and Administrative Support

This may be an accountant, a bookkeeper, or simply a trusted person who can help with bureaucratic tasks when I am depleted. The ASD/INTP executive function is vulnerable to collapse under administrative load. Having someone who can handle or guide me through tax issues, benefit applications, or complex paperwork is a form of sovereignty protection. This support can be paid or reciprocal.

The Protocol

  1. Identify gaps. For each category above, note whether I currently have a person identified and whether they are accessible. If the answer is "no" or "I don't know," that is the gap.
  2. Research and list candidates. For legal: search for local attorneys with relevant specialties (contract, tenant, criminal defense). For psychological: search for therapists with experience in ASD, INTP traits, or who practice cognitive-behavioral approaches. Write down names and contact information.
  3. Make initial contact before it's urgent. Schedule a brief consultation with a lawyer. Book an initial session with a therapist. The goal is not to solve a current problem. The goal is to establish the relationship so that when a problem arises, I am not starting from zero.
  4. Designate an emergency contact. Have a conversation with the person I choose. Explain what I would need from them in a crisis: to communicate on my behalf, to help with decisions, to be a buffer. Ensure they have essential contact information. This conversation may feel awkward. It is a form of care for both myself and for them.
  5. Create a crisis document. A single, accessible file containing: lawyer contact, therapist contact, emergency contact, basic financial and medical information. This document is for me or for my proxy. In a crisis, I do not need to remember where anything is. I open one file.
  6. Review and update annually. Support relationships change. People move, retire, or become unavailable. Once a year, review the crisis document. Update contacts. Confirm that the relationships are still active.

The Deeper Layer

Having support ready can feel like an admission of vulnerability to the 5. "I should be able to handle this myself. I should not need a lawyer or a therapist. That is for people who are weak or in trouble." This is the shadow of the 5's self-sufficiency narrative. The reality is that the most sovereign individuals have robust support structures. They do not rely on them daily, but they exist, maintained and ready. The person who insists they need no one is the person who is most alone when the crisis comes.

The Fe inferior may resist designating an emergency contact because it fears being a burden. "I can't ask someone to do that for me. What if they say no? What if they resent me?" This is a miscalibration. Most people are honored to be asked. They want to help but do not know how. A clear, specific request—"If I am ever incapacitated, can I list you as my emergency contact?"—is a gift of clarity. It tells them exactly what they can do.

The ASD dimension adds a practical barrier. Researching and contacting professionals is an executive function-heavy task. It is easy to defer indefinitely. The protocol is designed to break this into small, manageable steps. Identifying names is one step. Making one call is one step. The goal is not to have everything perfectly arranged. The goal is to have more than zero. One lawyer's name. One therapist's number. One person who knows they are my emergency contact. That is already a significant increase in resilience.

Reflection Questions

  • Do I have a lawyer identified? If not, what is the smallest step I can take toward having one?
  • Do I have a therapist or coach who understands my configuration? Am I actively engaged with them, or is the relationship dormant?
  • Who could serve as my emergency contact? Have I had the conversation with them?
  • What would it feel like to have these supports in place? How might it change my baseline anxiety about potential crises?
