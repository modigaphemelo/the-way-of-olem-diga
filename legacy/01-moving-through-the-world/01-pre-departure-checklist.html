Pre-Departure Checklist

What do I need before I leave my controlled space?

Why This Matters

Leaving the sanctuary is not a neutral act. It is a transition from a controlled, predictable, low-demand environment to an uncontrolled, unpredictable, high-demand one. For the ASD/INTP nervous system, this transition is metabolically expensive. The sensory landscape changes. The social demands activate. The executive function must manage navigation, timing, and unexpected events. Without preparation, I arrive at my destination already depleted, having spent the journey's energy before the journey has begun.

The pre-departure checklist is a ritualized sequence that ensures I leave the sanctuary with the tools I need and a clear understanding of the mission. It is the Si anchor for the transition from inside to outside. It reduces the cognitive load of departure by externalizing the list of requirements. I do not need to remember what to bring. I do not need to decide whether I am ready. I follow the list. When the list is complete, I leave. The checklist does not guarantee a successful outing. It guarantees that I am as prepared as possible, which is the only variable under my control.

The Checklist

1. Define the Mission Duration

I decide, before leaving, exactly how long I will be gone. "I will be at the event for two hours." "I will run errands for 90 minutes." The duration provides a container. It is the boundary that prevents the outing from expanding indefinitely and draining more energy than I have allocated. The duration is communicated to anyone who needs to know, and it is non-negotiable in the moment. If I wish to extend, I make a conscious decision to do so, not a passive drift.

2. Define the Mission Objective

I am not "going out." I am going to accomplish something specific. "I will acquire groceries from the list." "I will observe the social dynamics at the gathering for research purposes." "I will maintain the relationship with Person X by having a brief, engaged conversation." The objective gives the outing purpose. It engages the Ti function, which prefers a defined problem over an open-ended situation. The objective is not a demand for performance. It is a compass bearing.

3. Prepare the Sensory Kit

I place in my bag or pockets: noise-canceling headphones, a small textured object for grounding, sunglasses if bright light is expected, a water bottle, and any necessary medication. The sensory kit is the portable version of the sanctuary. Its items are selected because they reliably reduce sensory overwhelm. I do not leave without checking that each item is present.

4. Prepare the Exit Strategy

I know, before I leave, how I will extract myself if necessary. I have a pre-planned neutral exit phrase: "I need to head out. Good to see you." I know where the exits are. I have transportation arranged or a clear route home. The exit strategy is not paranoia. It is the knowledge that if my sensory or social capacity is exceeded, I can leave immediately and safely. This knowledge reduces baseline anxiety and makes it less likely that I will need to use it.

5. Communicate the Plan

If I live with others, I inform them briefly of my expected return time. If I am meeting someone, I confirm the details. The communication is factual, not apologetic. It sets expectations and prevents misunderstandings that would require additional cognitive energy to resolve later.

The Protocol

  1. Write the checklist on a physical card or a prominent digital note. The items are: mission duration, mission objective, sensory kit check, exit strategy, communication. The card is the external memory. I do not need to recall the items. I read them.
  2. Run through the checklist before every departure. This is not optional. The checklist is the ritual. It signals to the nervous system: "We are preparing to leave the sanctuary. Here is the plan." The ritual reduces the initiation cost of leaving.
  3. If the checklist cannot be completed, I do not leave. If I cannot define a duration or an objective, if the sensory kit is missing a critical item, if the exit strategy is unclear, the conditions for a successful outing are not met. I postpone or replan. Leaving unprepared is a predictable path to overwhelm.
  4. Review the checklist after returning. Was anything missing? What item was most useful? What would I add or remove? The checklist evolves with experience. The post-return review is the calibration.

The Deeper Layer

The pre-departure checklist can seem excessive. Neurotypical people do not need a written list to leave the house. They rely on internal memory and real-time adaptation. I cannot rely on those functions, or I can rely on them only intermittently and at high cost. The checklist is not a crutch for a broken system. It is a tool for a system that operates differently. It replaces unreliable internal processes with reliable external ones. It is an accommodation that makes the world accessible to a mind that was not designed for the world's default settings.

The 5w4 may resist the checklist because it feels like an admission of inadequacy. "I should be able to leave the house without a protocol." But the protocol is not for the person I should be. It is for the person I am. And the person I am, using this protocol, can navigate the world with less anxiety, less depletion, and more sovereignty than the person who insists on doing it the hard way. The checklist is not a limitation. It is a liberation.

Reflection Questions

  • What is currently in my bag or pockets when I leave the house? What items have I learned I cannot do without?
  • When have I left home without a clear plan and regretted it? What was the cost?
  • What is my current exit strategy for social events? Do I have a neutral phrase ready?
  • How would it feel to run through a pre-departure checklist without judgment, as a simple act of preparation?
