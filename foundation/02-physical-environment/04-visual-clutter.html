Practice 4 of 6

Visual Clutter

What enters my peripheral vision? Does it pull attention?

Why This Matters

My visual system is constantly scanning. For the ASD mind, peripheral vision is not a passive background. Every object, every stack of paper, every icon on the desktop registers as a discrete item that must be processed or intentionally ignored. The cognitive cost of "ignoring" is not zero. It is a continuous, low-grade tax on attention. I may not feel it in the moment, but I will feel it as fatigue after hours of work in a cluttered space.

Visual clutter is not merely physical mess. It is also digital: open tabs, desktop icons, notification badges, multiple windows. Each of these is a visual anchor that pulls attention away from the primary task. The goal is not a sterile, empty room. The goal is a visual field that contains only what is relevant to the current focus. Everything else is noise, and noise has a cost.

The Principles

Clear Surfaces

The desk is the primary visual field during work. Any object on the desk that is not directly related to the current task is a distraction. I do not need to become a minimalist. I need to create a clear zone for focus. When I begin a deep work session, the desk should contain only: the computer, the input devices, the water bottle, and perhaps one intentional sensory object (the Si anchor). Everything else should be removed from the immediate visual field. This can be as simple as pushing items to the far edges or placing them in a drawer.

Single Monitor, Single Task

Multiple monitors increase the visual field and invite multitasking. The ASD brain does not multitask well. It switches tasks with high cognitive cost. A single monitor, with a single application in focus, reduces the visual noise and forces attention to the primary task. If a second monitor is necessary for reference material, it should be positioned to the side, requiring a deliberate head turn to view. It should not be in the peripheral vision.

Closed Tabs and Applications

Every open browser tab is a visual anchor. Every application running in the background is a potential interruption. Before beginning a deep work block, I will close all tabs and applications that are not required for the session. This includes email, messaging, and any social media. The goal is a clean digital workspace that mirrors the clean physical workspace.

Desktop as a Blank Canvas

The computer desktop accumulates files like a physical desk accumulates papers. I will adopt a policy: the desktop contains zero files. Files live in organized folders. The desktop is a temporary workspace, cleared at the end of each day. This reduces the visual noise and removes the subtle anxiety of an ever-growing pile of unprocessed items.

Notification Elimination

Notifications are visual clutter that demand immediate attention. I will disable all non-essential notifications on all devices. Email badges, message popups, update alerts—all of these are interruptions disguised as information. The default state of my devices should be silence, both auditory and visual. I will check communications on my own schedule, not when the device decides I should.

The Protocol

  1. Audit current visual field. Sit in my normal work position. Look at the desk and the surrounding area. List every object I can see. For each object, ask: "Is this necessary for the task I am about to do?" If the answer is no, it is clutter.
  2. Clear the primary workspace. Remove all non-essential items from the desk before the next deep work session. Place them in a drawer, on a shelf behind me, or in another room. The goal is a clear surface within arm's reach.
  3. Close all non-essential digital items. Before starting work, close all browser tabs, quit all applications not needed for the session, and clear the desktop of files. Set the status to "Do Not Disturb."
  4. Adopt a "clear at close" habit. At the end of each work session or day, spend two minutes clearing the physical and digital workspace. This ensures the next session begins from a clean state. The two-minute investment returns focus for the entire next session.
  5. Audit the peripheral environment. What is visible behind the monitor? A cluttered shelf, a messy corner, a pile of items on the floor? These are outside the immediate focus but still register in peripheral vision. Over time, reduce the visual noise in the entire room. This is a long-term project, not an immediate demand.

The Deeper Layer

Visual clutter is often a physical manifestation of mental clutter. The piles of paper, the unsorted items, the icons on the desktop—these are external representations of internal tasks that have not been processed or completed. They carry a subtle psychological weight. Clearing them is not just about reducing sensory input. It is about signaling to the nervous system: "This space is for focus. The other things have been handled or deferred. You are free to attend to the present."

There is also a 5w4 dimension to this. The 4 wing craves an environment that feels intentional and aesthetically coherent. A cluttered space feels like a life uncurated. The 5 wants to conserve energy and avoid the overwhelm of too many inputs. A clean visual field satisfies both. It is an act of self-respect, a quiet assertion that my attention is valuable and worth protecting.

Reflection Questions

  • What is the most consistently distracting visual element in my current workspace? A specific pile? A particular screen icon?
  • How many browser tabs are typically open when I try to work? What would change if I closed them all before starting?
  • Does my desktop have files on it? What would it feel like to see a completely empty desktop?
  • What is one small change I can make to my visual environment this week that would reduce cognitive load?
