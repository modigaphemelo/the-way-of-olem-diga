Light Audit

What lighting conditions allow sustained focus?

Why This Matters

Light is not neutral. For the ASD sensory system, artificial lighting, especially fluorescent overhead lighting, is a continuous low-grade assault. Fluorescent lights flicker at a frequency that is technically imperceptible but neurologically registered. They emit a spectrum heavy in blue light, which signals "daytime" to the brain even when I need to focus inward or wind down. Overhead lighting creates glare on screens and casts harsh shadows. All of this adds to cognitive load without my conscious awareness.

I have spent years in environments with bad lighting, assuming my fatigue, headaches, and difficulty focusing were personal failings. They were not. They were predictable responses to a hostile sensory environment. Controlling light is one of the highest-leverage changes I can make to my workspace. It costs little and returns immediate gains in focus duration and comfort.

The Principles

Single Source

Multiple light sources create competing shadows and visual complexity. A single source, ideally positioned to the side or behind the screen, creates a calm visual field. If I must use multiple sources, they should be dim and indirect. The goal is to eliminate the "interrogation room" effect of evenly distributed overhead light.

Warm Temperature

Light temperature is measured in Kelvin. Cool light (above 4000K) is blue-heavy and stimulating. Warm light (2700K to 3000K) is amber and calming. For focus work that is analytical rather than urgent, warm light is superior. It signals the nervous system that this is a space for sustained attention, not for fight-or-flight. I should use bulbs labeled "warm white" or "soft white." Smart bulbs that allow temperature adjustment are ideal.

Dimmable

Optimal brightness varies with time of day and task. I need the ability to adjust. A dimmer switch or a smart bulb is not a luxury. It is a precision tool for managing sensory input. On days when I am already overstimulated, I can lower the light further. On days when I need alertness, I can raise it slightly. The control itself is calming.

No Overhead Fluorescents

If my workspace has overhead fluorescent tubes, I must either disable them (remove the bulbs, cover the fixture) or never use them. The flicker and spectrum are non-negotiable drains. If I cannot control the overhead lighting (e.g., in an office I do not own), I need countermeasures: a desk lamp as the primary source, a hat with a brim to block the overhead light, and positioning myself so the overhead is behind me, not in my line of sight.

Screen Light Management

The light from my screens is part of the equation. I should use dark mode on all applications that support it. I should reduce blue light emission in the evening using built-in features (Night Shift, Night Light) or software like f.lux. I should position screens so that no window or bright light source reflects off them.

The Protocol

  1. Audit current lighting. Spend five minutes in my primary workspace with the lights as they normally are. Note: What is the primary source? Is it overhead, desk lamp, natural? What is the color temperature? Is it dimmable? Does it create glare on my screen?
  2. Eliminate the worst offender. If there is an overhead fluorescent, turn it off and use an alternative. If there is a harsh LED bulb, replace it with a warm white bulb. Make one change this week.
  3. Acquire a single warm, dimmable source. A simple desk lamp with a warm LED bulb and a dimmer (or smart bulb) is the baseline. Position it to the side, not directly facing me. This becomes the primary work light.
  4. Test and adjust. For one week, use only the warm, dimmable source for focused work. Adjust brightness based on energy level. Note any difference in focus duration, eye strain, or end-of-day fatigue.

The Deeper Layer

Light is often the first sensory dimension I neglect because it seems trivial. It is not. The ASD nervous system does not habituate to sensory input the way a neurotypical system does. The flicker of a fluorescent tube is processed anew every moment. The cognitive cost accumulates silently. By controlling light, I am not being precious. I am removing a constant, unnecessary tax on my attention.

There is also a psychological dimension. Warm, dim light signals "sanctuary." It creates a boundary between the overstimulating outside world and the focused interior. This is not about aesthetics. It is about creating a physical cue that tells my nervous system: "You are in the focused space now. You can let the vigilance drop." The light is part of the Si anchor system described in the previous module.

Reflection Questions

  • What is the primary light source in my current workspace? What is its temperature and position?
  • Have I ever noticed feeling more tired or headachy after working under a particular light? Did I connect the cause and effect at the time?
  • If I could change one thing about my workspace lighting today, what would it be?
  • What is the quality of light in the space where I feel most calm and focused? How can I replicate elements of it?
