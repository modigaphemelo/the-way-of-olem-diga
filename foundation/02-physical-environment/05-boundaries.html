Practice 5 of 6

Boundaries

How do I signal "do not disturb" to others?

Why This Matters

Deep work requires uninterrupted time. For the ASD/INTP mind, an interruption is not a minor inconvenience. It is a full cognitive reset. The model I was building in my head collapses. Rebuilding it takes fifteen to thirty minutes of additional focus time, assuming I can re-enter the flow state at all. Each interruption is not a momentary pause. It is a significant loss of productive capacity.

Most people do not understand this. They operate on a different interruption tolerance. They can pause a conversation, answer a question, and resume without losing their place. I cannot. This is not a failure of politeness. It is a neurological difference. The burden of managing interruptions falls on me. I must create clear, unambiguous signals that communicate: "I am not available right now." This is not rude. It is protecting my primary function.

The Principles

Physical Boundary: The Door

If I have a door, it should be closed during deep work. A closed door is a universal signal. It requires no explanation. If I do not have a door, or if closing it feels too isolating, I need a visible proxy. A small sign on the desk, a specific object placed in a visible location, or even wearing obvious headphones (even without sound) can serve as the boundary marker.

Digital Boundary: Status Indicators

My digital presence should mirror my physical unavailability. "Do Not Disturb" mode on all devices. Status set to "Focusing" or offline. Auto-replies on email or messaging that indicate when I will next check messages. The goal is to manage expectations. If people know I will respond in three hours, they are less likely to expect an immediate reply.

Scheduled Unavailability

The most effective boundary is one communicated in advance. I should have a recurring schedule for deep work blocks. I communicate this schedule to anyone who might need to reach me: "I am generally unavailable between 9am and 1pm. I will respond after that." This removes the ambiguity. They are not being ignored. They are being deferred to a known time.

Scripted Responses for Boundary Violations

Boundaries will be tested. Someone will knock on the closed door. A message will come through marked "urgent." I need a prepared, neutral response that reinforces the boundary without escalating conflict. "I'm in the middle of something. Can this wait until [specific time]?" If it truly cannot wait, I have a protocol for handling the interruption (see Emergency Reset). If it can wait, I have protected my focus without damaging the relationship.

The Internal Boundary

The most frequent interrupter is my own mind. The urge to check a notification, to open a new tab, to "just quickly" do something else. This is the Ne drift. The physical and digital boundaries are also signals to myself: "You are in the focus space now. Wandering is not permitted." The boundary is as much for me as it is for others.

The Protocol

  1. Define my deep work schedule. Identify the recurring blocks of time I will protect. Write them down. Make them visible in my calendar. This is the first boundary: a commitment to myself.
  2. Communicate the schedule to relevant people. A simple message: "I'm setting aside [time blocks] for focused work. I'll be offline during those hours and will catch up after." This sets expectations.
  3. Establish a physical boundary marker. If I have a door, close it. If not, identify a visible object or sign that signals "do not disturb." Place it in position at the start of each deep work block.
  4. Activate digital boundaries. Enable "Do Not Disturb" across all devices. Set an auto-responder if appropriate. Close all communication applications.
  5. Prepare the boundary script. Write down exactly what I will say if someone interrupts despite the signals. Practice it once. "I'm in a focus block until [time]. Can we talk then?"
  6. Review and adjust weekly. Did the boundaries hold? Were there unexpected interruptions? Adjust the schedule, the signals, or the communication for the following week.

The Deeper Layer

Setting boundaries activates the Fe inferior's fear of rejection. "If I say no, they will think I am difficult. They will stop liking me. They will leave." This fear is real. It is also often inaccurate. Clear, consistent boundaries, communicated without apology, actually increase respect. They signal that I value my time and my work. People who cannot respect a reasonable boundary are often people whose demands would have drained me regardless.

There is also a 5w4 dimension. The 5 hoards time and energy. Boundaries are the fence around the hoard. Without the fence, the hoard is constantly raided, and the 5 lives in a state of perpetual depletion and resentment. The 4 wing needs the work to feel sacred. Boundaries create the sacred space. They declare: "This time, this work, this focus is important."

The internal boundary is the hardest to hold. The Ne drift is seductive. It promises novelty and relief from the difficulty of sustained focus. The boundary signals, especially the physical ones, serve as a counterweight. They are external reminders of the commitment I made to myself. They are the architecture of discipline.

Reflection Questions

  • What is my current deep work schedule? Is it consistent and protected, or ad hoc and easily interrupted?
  • What physical signal do I currently use to indicate unavailability? Is it effective?
  • Who most frequently interrupts my focus? Is it external people, or my own Ne drift?
  • What is one boundary I can establish this week that would protect a specific deep work block?
