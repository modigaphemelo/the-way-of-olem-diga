Practice 4 of 6

Energy Economics

What is my daily battery capacity? What drains it fastest?

Why This Matters

I have a finite amount of functional energy each day. This is not a metaphor. My specific neurotype processes sensory input, social interaction, and executive tasks at a higher metabolic cost than a neurotypical brain. I start each day with a battery that is smaller than most, and it drains faster. When the battery is empty, I cannot think clearly, regulate emotions, or initiate action. I am not being dramatic. I am describing a neurological reality.

Most productivity advice assumes a renewable energy source. It assumes that with enough motivation, breaks, and self-care, I can operate at full capacity for eight to ten hours a day. This is false for me. My functional window is narrower. Understanding the exact size of my battery, the specific activities that drain it fastest, and the true cost of recovery is not self-limitation. It is accurate resource management. I cannot budget time if I do not budget energy first.

The Drain Categories

Masking Cost

Masking is the act of performing neurotypical social behavior: maintaining eye contact, modulating tone, suppressing stims, reading and responding to social cues in real time, and filtering what I say through a layer of appropriateness. This is not automatic for me. It is a conscious, cognitively expensive simulation running on my main processor. Two hours of active masking can drain more energy than six hours of deep work. A full day of masking (e.g., a conference, a family gathering) can require two to three days of recovery. I must track masking as a discrete, high-cost activity, not lump it in with general "socializing."

Sensory Load

As documented in Practice 2, my sensory bandwidth is narrow. Fluorescent lights, open-plan noise, unpredictable sounds, and visual clutter all drain the battery continuously, even when I am not consciously aware of them. This is a passive drain. I can be doing nothing cognitively demanding and still lose 40% of my battery to a hostile sensory environment. The cost is invisible until the crash.

Executive Function Tasks

Initiation, transitions, and decisions all consume energy. Each time I must switch contexts, start a new task from a standstill, or choose between ambiguous options, I spend from the same limited reserve. A day filled with many small tasks and frequent interruptions drains far more than a day with one deep work block, even if the total "work hours" are identical.

Emotional Processing

Conflict, criticism, uncertainty about relationships, and the Fe inferior's constant low-level scanning for social threat all consume energy. I may not notice this drain because it operates in the background, but it accumulates. An unresolved tension with someone close to me can halve my functional capacity until it is addressed or I consciously quarantine it.

Hyperfocus Hangover

When I enter a deep Ti flow state, I can work for hours without noticing fatigue. The energy expenditure is real, but it is deferred. When I finally surface, I crash. This is the hyperfocus hangover. It is not a sign that I did something wrong. It is the cost of operating at that depth. I must budget for the recovery period as part of the work, not as an afterthought.

The Protocol

  1. Track one week of energy data. For seven days, keep a simple log. Every few hours, note: current energy level (1 to 10), what I have been doing, and which drain categories were active. Be honest. The goal is data, not judgment.
  2. Identify my daily battery capacity. Review the log. On an average day, how many hours of functional energy do I actually have? Not how many I wish I had. How many I have. This is my baseline. Most people in my configuration have between three and five hours of true functional capacity per day. The rest is maintenance, recovery, and low-demand activity.
  3. Calculate the cost of key activities. How much battery does a one-hour meeting consume? A two-hour deep work block? A family dinner? Assign rough percentages or hour equivalents. Example: "One hour of active masking equals three hours of battery drain."
  4. Build a weekly energy budget. Using the capacity and costs, plan the week around energy, not time. Schedule high-cost activities sparingly. Protect recovery blocks as non-negotiable. Accept that on days with unavoidable high drain (e.g., a social obligation), productive work may be impossible, and that is not a failure of discipline.

The Deeper Layer

Energy economics requires accepting a limitation that feels like a personal failure. I have internalized the cultural message that capacity is a function of effort and character. It is not. Capacity is neurological. I cannot will myself to have a larger battery. I can only manage the one I have with precision.

This acceptance is particularly difficult for the 5w4. The 5 fears being depleted and overwhelmed, so it hoards energy. The 4 wing wants to believe in its own exceptionalism, that it can transcend limits through intensity and vision. Neither strategy works. Hoarding leads to isolation and a life unlived. Transcendence fantasies lead to repeated crashes and self-recrimination. The only viable path is accurate accounting and deliberate allocation.

There will be days when I exceed the budget. There will be days when an unexpected drain empties the battery by noon. The protocol for those days is not to push through. It is to declare an energy emergency, reduce all non-essential demands, and rest. Pushing through an empty battery borrows from tomorrow at punitive interest rates.

Reflection Questions

  • What is my actual functional capacity on an average day, in hours? Am I surprised by the number?
  • Which drain category costs me the most that I was not previously accounting for? (Masking? Sensory load? Emotional processing?)
  • What activity do I consistently underestimate the recovery cost of?
  • What would I remove or reduce from my week if I treated my energy budget as non-negotiable?
  • How do I typically respond when I hit empty? Is it effective, or does it create more debt?
