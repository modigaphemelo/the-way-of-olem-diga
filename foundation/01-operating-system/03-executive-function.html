Executive Function Notes

Where do I get stuck? Transitions, initiation, decision making, and processing lag.

Why This Matters

Executive function is the set of cognitive processes that allow me to plan, initiate, sustain, and switch between tasks. For the Level 1 ASD mind, these processes do not operate smoothly or reliably. I can know exactly what needs to be done, want to do it, and still remain motionless. This is not laziness. It is not a character defect. It is autistic inertia, a well documented difficulty with task initiation and state transitions.

Standard advice assumes an intact executive engine: "Just start." "Break it down." "Use a timer." These strategies assume that the barrier is cognitive (not knowing how) or motivational (not wanting to). For me, the barrier is often purely executive: the signal from intention to action does not reliably transmit. Understanding where my executive function breaks down, and under what conditions, is essential. Without this map, I will continue to blame myself for failures that are neurological, not moral.

The Friction Points

Task Initiation

The hardest part of any task is starting it. I can sit for hours knowing I need to begin something, watching the time disappear, feeling the pressure build, and still not move. This is not procrastination in the typical sense. It is a failure of the ignition system. The engine is fine. The starter motor is unreliable.

What helps: External momentum (a person expecting me, a scheduled call, a commitment I cannot quietly break), reducing the cognitive load of the first step to a pure physical action (open the document, put on shoes), and body first protocols (stand up, walk to another room) that bypass the cognitive loop entirely.

Transitions

Moving from one activity or state to another is disproportionately costly. Leaving the house requires a full mental gear shift. Stopping deep work to attend a meeting feels violent. Even positive transitions (finishing work to relax) can be jarring. I get stuck in whatever mode I am currently in.

What helps: Rituals that mark transitions (a specific song, making tea, a short walk), buffer time between activities (never schedule back to back), and accepting that transitions will always cost something and budgeting for that cost.

Decision Making

Decisions, especially open-ended ones with many variables, drain executive function rapidly. Choosing what to eat, what to work on first, or how to respond to an ambiguous email can consume disproportionate energy. This is decision fatigue, and I experience it more acutely than most.

What helps: Reducing decisions by creating defaults (same breakfast every day, a uniform of similar clothes), making decisions in advance (planning the next day the night before), and using structured decision frameworks (pro/con lists, timeboxing) to limit open-ended rumination.

Processing Lag

I do not process complex information, especially social or emotional information, in real time. There is a 48 to 72 hour delay between input and full integration. In the moment, I may feel nothing, seem detached, or give a response that later feels inadequate. The real response, the one that reflects my actual understanding and feelings, arrives days later.

What helps: Explicitly requesting time to process ("I need to think about this; I'll get back to you in a few days"), not forcing myself to give immediate responses to significant matters, and journaling to track the delayed processing so I can return to important conversations once integration is complete.

The Protocol

  1. Map my friction points. For one week, keep a simple log. When I get stuck, note: what I was trying to do, which friction point was active (initiation, transition, decision, or lag), and what (if anything) finally got me unstuck. Look for patterns.
  2. Build an external momentum system. Identify at least one reliable external ignition source: a scheduled call, a commitment to another person, a public deadline. Use these intentionally to break inertia on important tasks.
  3. Create transition rituals. Design a short, repeatable sequence that signals a mode shift. Example: before deep work, make tea, put on headphones, clear desk. Before leaving the house, check keys, wallet, phone, then stand at the door for ten seconds. The ritual tells the brain: "We are changing states now."
  4. Implement the 72-hour rule for significant responses. When faced with a complex decision or an emotionally charged communication, the default response is: "I need to process this. I will get back to you in a few days." This is not avoidance. It is respecting my actual processing timeline.

The Deeper Layer

Executive dysfunction is often invisible to others. They see someone who is smart, capable, and seemingly unmotivated. They do not see the internal freeze, the signal that never reaches the motor, the cognitive effort required for what appears effortless to them. I have internalized this judgment for years, believing I was lazy or undisciplined. Releasing that narrative is part of this practice.

The goal is not to "fix" my executive function and become someone who initiates effortlessly. That is not a realistic outcome. The goal is to design a life that works with my executive profile: a life with fewer transitions, more external structure, more pre-made decisions, and explicit permission to take the time I need to process. This is accommodation, not weakness. It is building the ramp for a mind that does not do stairs.

Reflection Questions

  • Which friction point (initiation, transitions, decisions, processing lag) costs me the most energy on a typical day?
  • What external momentum sources have reliably worked for me in the past? How can I schedule them more intentionally?
  • What transition ritual can I implement this week for the state shift I find hardest (e.g., starting work, stopping work, leaving home)?
  • When have I forced an immediate response and later regretted it? What would have changed if I had used the 72-hour rule?
  • What is one decision I can eliminate entirely by creating a default? (e.g., what I eat for breakfast, what I wear, when I exercise.)
