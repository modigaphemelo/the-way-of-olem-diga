Practice 5 of 6

External Support Dynamics

Who or what provides external momentum? What happens when absent?

Why This Matters

I cannot reliably generate internal momentum. This is not a character flaw. It is a documented feature of autistic inertia combined with INTP executive function. The ignition system is unreliable. I can want to do something, know exactly how to do it, understand the consequences of not doing it, and still remain motionless. This is the gap between intention and action that Practice 3 documented. The most reliable bridge across that gap is not more willpower. It is external support.

External support takes two primary forms: Fe Anchors and Si Anchors. Fe Anchors are people who provide momentum through expectation, presence, or accountability. Si Anchors are objects, rituals, or environmental cues that trigger action without requiring internal initiation. Both are necessary. Over-reliance on Fe Anchors creates dependency and vulnerability when the anchor is absent. Under-development of Si Anchors leaves me stranded when alone. Understanding and balancing these two types of support is essential for sustainable function.

Fe Anchors (People as Momentum)

An Fe Anchor is someone whose expectation or presence pulls me into action. This can be direct (a scheduled meeting I cannot miss) or indirect (knowing someone will ask about my progress). The mechanism is the inferior Fe's sensitivity to external validation and its discomfort with letting others down. When harnessed intentionally, this is a powerful tool. When unconscious, it becomes a dependency.

Characteristics of a healthy Fe Anchor: The relationship is not purely transactional. There is genuine mutual care. The anchor understands my wiring and does not shame me for needing external structure. They are reliable but not overbearing. They provide the minimum necessary push, not constant management.

Signs of unhealthy Fe Anchor dependency: I cannot initiate without a specific person present. My mood and productivity collapse when they are unavailable. I resent them for the power they hold over my function. I have not developed any internal or Si-based alternatives.

The vulnerability: Fe Anchors are human. They get sick, they have their own lives, they may leave. When a primary Fe Anchor is absent, I experience a predictable crash. The campus problem described earlier, the inability to leave the room when a certain person is not there, is a classic Fe Anchor dependency crisis. The solution is not to eliminate Fe Anchors. They are valuable. The solution is to diversify and to build Si Anchors as backup.

Si Anchors (Objects and Rituals as Momentum)

An Si Anchor is a sensory or routine-based trigger that initiates action without requiring social expectation. It works because the brain has been conditioned to associate a specific cue with a specific action. Over time, encountering the cue reduces the executive function cost of starting.

Examples of Si Anchors: A specific playlist that I only play when starting deep work. A particular mug that signals "work mode." Laying out clothes the night before so the morning requires no decisions. A sticky note on my monitor with a single word that cues a protocol. A weighted blanket that, when placed on my lap, signals "focus time." The act of making tea in a specific way that bridges the gap between waking and working.

Developing Si Anchors: This requires deliberate, repeated pairing. Choose a cue that is easy to deploy and not used for other purposes. Pair it consistently with the desired action. Do this for at least two weeks before expecting the anchor to hold. The goal is for the cue itself to reduce the initiation threshold, so that when I am stuck, I can reach for the anchor rather than waiting for internal motivation.

Si Anchors as emergency backup: When my primary Fe Anchor is absent, I need a pre-planned Si Anchor sequence. Example: "When I cannot initiate leaving the house, I will (1) put on the specific playlist, (2) place my weighted object on my lap for two minutes, (3) stand up and put on shoes. The sequence is the anchor." This bypasses the need for internal decision-making.

The Protocol

  1. Identify my current Fe Anchors. List the people whose presence or expectation reliably helps me initiate action. For each, note: What specific actions do they help me start? What happens to my function when they are absent? Is the dependency healthy or unhealthy?
  2. Identify one area of high friction. Where do I most consistently get stuck when alone? (e.g., leaving the house, starting work, stopping work, making phone calls.)
  3. Design one Si Anchor for that friction point. Choose a specific sensory cue or short ritual. Write it down as a sequence. Example: "To start work: (1) Make tea in the grey mug. (2) Put on 'Deep Focus' playlist. (3) Open the document. (4) Write one sentence." The anchor is the entire sequence, not just the first step.
  4. Practice the anchor when I do not need it. For one week, use the Si Anchor even on days when initiation is easy. This builds the association. The anchor must be reliable before it is needed in an emergency.
  5. Create an emergency protocol for Fe Anchor absence. Write a specific sequence to follow when my primary person is unavailable and I am stuck. This is a document I can access without thinking. It tells me exactly what to do.

The Deeper Layer

There is shame in needing external support. The 5w4 wants to be self-sufficient, to operate from pure internal sovereignty. Admitting that I cannot reliably initiate alone feels like a fundamental failure. It is not. It is accurate self-knowledge. Every human uses external structure. Neurotypical people use calendars, deadlines, social pressure, and routines. They just do not notice because the structures are culturally invisible. My needs are more specific, not more shameful.

The goal is not to eliminate Fe Anchors. Connection is a legitimate human need, and for the Fe inferior, it is a legitimate functional support. The goal is to build a diversified portfolio of supports: Fe Anchors for connection and accountability, Si Anchors for solo function, and the internal knowledge that when all else fails, I have a written protocol to follow. Sovereignty is not needing no one. It is having options when any single support is absent.

Reflection Questions

  • Who are my primary Fe Anchors? What happens to my ability to initiate when they are not available?
  • What is one specific situation where I consistently fail to initiate alone? What would a Si Anchor for that situation look like?
  • Have I ever resented a Fe Anchor for the dependency I felt? What was that about?
  • What small ritual or object already helps me transition or start tasks, even if I have not named it as an anchor?
  • What is one sentence I could write on a sticky note and place in my environment that would cue a helpful action?
